# Executive Strategy Patterns

Use this reference when the user wants a more structured strategic diagnosis, a boss-facing memo, or a sharper set of strategic options.

## Choose The Right Lens

### 1. Growth has stalled

Use this sequence:

1. Check whether the problem is demand, conversion, delivery capacity, retention, or pricing.
2. Identify the most profitable customer segment, not just the fastest-growing one.
3. Test whether growth should come from focus, channel change, product packaging, or pricing rather than new business lines.

Good boss-facing wording:

- "The issue is not lack of opportunity; it is diluted focus."
- "Growth is being constrained more by conversion economics than by market size."
- "Before opening a second curve, fix the first curve's repeatability."

### 2. Too many businesses or initiatives

Use this sequence:

1. Separate core cash engine from optional bets.
2. Rank initiatives by strategic importance, economic quality, and management bandwidth required.
3. Cut, pause, or delegate anything that consumes leadership attention without creating compounding advantage.

Good boss-facing wording:

- "The company has a portfolio problem, not an effort problem."
- "Management attention is the scarcest resource here."
- "The priority is not adding more projects, but reducing strategic noise."

### 3. Market entry or expansion

Use this sequence:

1. Define why this market matters now.
2. Check whether the company has an unfair advantage in product, channel, brand, regulation, or delivery.
3. Estimate the real cost of entry, including founder time.
4. Decide whether to enter, partner, test lightly, or wait.

Good boss-facing wording:

- "This is attractive in theory but expensive in management attention."
- "Enter only with a wedge, not with a full-platform plan."
- "If we cannot define the first narrow win, we are not ready to enter."

### 4. Price pressure or competitive attack

Use this sequence:

1. Distinguish a pricing problem from a value communication problem.
2. Identify what customers would still pay for even if the competitor were cheaper.
3. Decide whether to defend share, reposition upward, bundle, or withdraw.

Good boss-facing wording:

- "Matching price is the fastest way to destroy the wrong battle."
- "The response should protect margin quality, not just volume."
- "We should only fight where we can win without training the market to expect lower prices."

### 5. Turnaround or cash pressure

Use this sequence:

1. Stabilize cash and delivery reliability first.
2. Protect the customer and product lines that keep trust and gross profit alive.
3. Sequence recovery: stop the bleeding, restore control, then rebuild growth.

Good boss-facing wording:

- "This is a survival sequencing problem before it is a growth problem."
- "The company needs control before ambition."
- "Cash preservation is the strategy until strategic flexibility returns."

## Option Design Checklist

When presenting options, make sure each option changes at least one of these:

- Target customer
- Core offer
- Channel model
- Pricing model
- Resource allocation
- Operating focus
- Risk exposure

If two options differ only in wording, merge them.

## Boss-Facing Memo Templates

### A. Strategy Memo

```md
Bottom line:
<single-sentence judgment>

Core diagnosis:
- <what is structurally true>
- <what is changing outside the company>
- <what is internally limiting performance>

Options:
1. <option>; upside: <...>; trade-off: <...>; risk: <...>
2. <option>; upside: <...>; trade-off: <...>; risk: <...>
3. <option>; upside: <...>; trade-off: <...>; risk: <...>

Recommendation:
- <recommended path>
- Why now: <...>

Execution priorities:
- Next 30 days: <...>
- Next 90 days: <...>
- Next 180 days: <...>

Signals to monitor:
- <...>
- <...>
```

### B. Annual Planning Note

```md
Strategic theme for the year:
<one sentence>

What we must protect:
- <cash engine / customers / delivery / margin>

What we must change:
- <channel / org / product focus / pricing / cost structure>

Top 3 company bets:
- <bet 1>
- <bet 2>
- <bet 3>

What not to do this year:
- <initiatives to decline, defer, or stop>

CEO focus:
- <what the boss must personally drive>
```

### C. Decision Brief

```md
Decision:
<what must be decided>

Recommendation:
<yes / no / delay / test>

Why:
- <economic logic>
- <timing logic>
- <capability logic>

Conditions for success:
- <...>

Reason to stop:
- <trigger that invalidates the decision>
```

## What The Boss Should Personally Own

Usually keep the boss close to:

- Strategic narrative and major trade-offs
- Capital allocation and senior hiring
- Top customer, partner, board, or investor trust
- Cross-functional conflicts that block the core business
- The few metrics that define whether the strategy is working

Usually push execution detail downward unless the team is too weak or the business is in a turnaround.

## Warning Signs Of Weak Strategy Advice

Rewrite the answer if it:

- Recommends growth without naming the growth engine
- Suggests diversification before the core is repeatable
- Mentions execution problems but gives no strategic implication
- Ignores cash, margin, or management bandwidth
- Avoids making a recommendation
- Sounds impressive but gives the boss no concrete choice
